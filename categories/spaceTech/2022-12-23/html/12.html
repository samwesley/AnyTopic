Embry-Riddle Research Park hoping to expand onto city land

Eileen Zaffiro-Kean
The Daytona Beach News-Journal
View Comments

DAYTONA BEACH — In just six years, a grassy field in the southeastern corner of the Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University campus has transformed into a cutting edge research park with more than 100,000 square feet of innovation, research and development space focused on the aviation, technology and aerospace industries.

The Embry-Riddle Research Park now generates more than $135 million in economic impact every year. It has also created more than 210 internships and 163 jobs with an annual average salary of $75,000, well above the Daytona Beach area's average pay.

Now the university wants to expand the flourishing venture onto 3.5 acres of city land directly south of the research park, and eventually add more city land just south of that 3.5 acres.

"We just see incredible opportunities," said Embry-Riddle President Barry Butler. "Very few communities have this type of opportunity by an airport for this type of research. It's an industry that's going crazy with growth."

Daytona Beach city commissioners haven't voted yet on whether to give the land to Embry-Riddle. But when the idea was discussed at their Dec. 7 meeting, at least a few of them seemed ready to transfer the property.

"It's amazing what you guys are doing," said City Commissioner Stacy Cantu.

"For us this is a no-brainer," said Mayor Derrick Henry. "We appreciate the partnership. This project is another example of how we elevate the city's median income and what you bring to the community."

A formal agreement will be placed on a City Commission agenda in the coming months. In the meantime, progress is surging ahead in the research park that fronts Clyde Morris Boulevard just north of the Bellevue Avenue Extension.

'A very fast trajectory' for ERAU research park

The Embry-Riddle Research Park began with the 50,000-square-foot John Mica Aviation and Aerospace Innovation Complex that opened in April 2017 and has grown quickly.

The newest addition will be the Cici & Hyatt Brown Center for Aerospace Technology, a 65,000-square-foot complex that was announced in March. A rendering shows a tall, modern building with floor-to-ceiling glass walls and nicely landscaped outdoor space with seating.

The Browns are donating $25 million for the aerospace technology building, the largest single gift in the university's history. The state is matching the Brown's contribution with another $25 million to fund the $50 million project.

"We've been on a very fast trajectory," Butler said.

The Embry-Riddle Research Park has attracted five international companies and brought more than $100 million in outside funding to Volusia County, Embry-Riddle Senior Vice President and Chief Operating Officer Rodney Cruise wrote in a recent letter to the city government.

"It's one of only three business research parks in the country that has airport access," Butler said. "It's an incredibly effective incubator."

Beyond the economic impact, the university hopes the research park becomes an inspiration for local children to study science, technology, engineering and math.

Future of the Embry-Riddle Research Park

Because the research park has been successful, the site is quickly reaching capacity. To maintain the momentum and growth, Embry-Riddle is asking the city to donate its 3.5-acre property on the northwest corner of Clyde Morris Boulevard and the Bellevue Avenue Extension.

The university wants the land to become home to a 45,000-square-foot building that would have space dedicated to furthering STEM inspiration for Volusia County Schools. Embry-Riddle hasn't shared any other details of what would be in the building.

The city land has an underground contaminate that the city has been working on cleaning up for years. The site is fenced off and there are ongoing environmental remediation efforts, but Embry-Riddle still wants the land.

"In consideration of the (land) donation, ERAU proposes to accept the parcel 'as is' and assume all remaining liability for the contamination, including costs of remediation," Cruise wrote in his letter to the city.

Butler said Embry-Riddle will ask the county government to contribute to cleanup costs.

The city already lets the university keep damaged plane parts on the land to use the site as a place for students to learn about plane crashes. Embry-Riddle said it could easily find a new spot for a crash lab.

Butler told commissioners at the Dec. 7 meeting that Embry-Riddle is also interested in the city property on the southwest corner of Clyde Morris Boulevard and the Bellevue Avenue Extension. It's a vacant site, but up until a few years ago the city had allowed homeless people to sleep there at night. The city even had portable toilets on the land for a while.

    Expert says robots could increase precision of surgeries to nearly 100 per cent

    With over 300 successful robotic surgeries conducted since its inception in June 2022, AASRA Group Managing Director and founder Dr. Jagadish J Hiremath says that robotic surgeries are a boon for patients thanks to its precision perfect methods and no scope for error. As AASRA is geared up to conduct another 2,000 surgeries next year, Dr Hiremath discusses A-Z of robotic surgeries and how it has become a blessing for patients. Advanced Academy for Surgery, Robotics, and Arthroplasty (AASRA) is the first hospital in Karnataka to use MAKO Smart robotics and second to use CMR surgical robotic system in the state. Dr Hiremath talks to Melvin Mathew to elucidate more on this.

    Is robot-assisted surgery the future of medicine?
    “To achieve perfection in surgeries, the human skill required by the surgeon needs to be taken out of the equation. In conventional surgeries, standard deviation due to human error has a 14-20 per cent rate.
    We want to drastically reduce the numbers, especially in major surgeries. Robot-assisted surgeries can increase the precision of surgeries to nearly 100 per cent.
    Slowly, tech-assisted surgeries will be the future. The future will involve robots conducting surgeries with surgeons overlooking them.”

    How did you get into robotics?
    “I have always been a technology-savvy doctor and have been associated with medical technology for a long time. Exposed to this side of the industry for quite some time, you know the new revolutions in the industry.
    As a practitioner, I have always tried to work with R&D teams to bring out newer technologies in the medical field. We became aware of robotics in early 2010 and its impact in patient care.
    When I came back to Bangalore in 2017, I noticed that robotics-assisted surgery was quite unaffordable and not covered by health insurance. Hence, we decided to revolutionise the space by providing robotics surgery at cost with conventional surgery.”

    Where do you borrow thetechnology from?
    “We are importing the robotics system from a US-based company Mako Robotics.
    We were the first to bring and use this in Karnataka but more than a million surgeries have been conducted through this technology. The Mako model used is the second generation one, and the first installation was in Hyderabad.

    How is robotic-assisted surgery better than conventional surgery?
    “A conventional surgery depends on the surgeon’s experience and skill. None of the robot assisted surgeries are completely automatic. We first assess the patient and send for 3D reconstruction of the knee joint using CT scan.
    After building the 3D model, it’s fed into the software. Assisted by robot specialists, the surgery plan is prepared and fed into the robot. The bio-medical engineer assists the surgeon with determining the cut areas.
    The sizing of the joints is pre-decided before the operation. Further, the machine guides the surgeon for the best course for the surgery and does not allow the surgeon to deviate from it.
    The surgeon operating with four-robotic arms can visualise the minor incisions with high clarity. In case of deviations, the machine shuts down immediately.
    The process can also help surgeons achieve near perfection by learning from surgeries.”

    How many different kinds of robots are available?
    “There are different robots for different departments. We have a different robot for urology, GI, pancreatic, cancer, and lung surgeries, and another one for hip and knee replacement surgeries. We are getting a third one for neuro and spine surgeries. By the end of January, we hope to be a completely robotic surgery team. We are also planning to bring in Cath lab interventions and Renal Artery Denervation in severe hypertension etc.
    What was the most complex surgery performed using this method?
    “Recently, we performed a hip-replacement surgery on a polio patient. We also operated on a patient with dual cancer. We also performed a complex lobectomy surgery using Versius platform from CMR as there was no prior database for the operation. We also performed a couple of Whipple surgeries using a robotics-assisted surgery system.”

    .



    Can the surgery time be reduced?

    “While initially the robot-assisted surgery might consume more time, eventually once the surgeon gets used to the procedure, the surgery time can be reduced to 90 per cent of time consumed by conventional methods.”

    Why is there hesitation in adopting the technology into the field?
    “While it leads to extremely successful results, the cost of the technology is very high. On an average, each unit costs Rs 20-30 crore. But this is the cost of early adoption of technology and is expected to drop gradually over the years.”


    Is the post-recovery time drastically reduced for patients?
    “Normally, patients start walking right after surgery, and by day four, return to normalcy. Compared to conventional surgery, the post-operative period is reduced by a greater degree. Morbidity and fatigue in patients is reduced to nearly zero.
